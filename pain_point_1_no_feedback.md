# Pain Point #1: Complete Absence of Feedback

## Overview

**The Problem**: Advisors who don't respond to emails, ignore draft submissions for months, or provide no feedback at all on student work.

**Severity**: **HIGHEST** - Career and financial consequences

**Prevalence**: Common complaint across all major graduate student forums (r/GradSchool, GradCafe, Academia Stack Exchange)

---

## The Problem in Detail

### What Students Experience

Students report advisors who:
- **Ignore emails for months** containing thesis drafts
- **Never respond** to draft submissions at all
- **Instruct students to submit** without providing any feedback on content
- **Avoid meetings** or cancel repeatedly
- **Provide no guidance** on whether work is acceptable

## What Good Feedback Looks Like (The Solution)

### Timeliness Standards

**Minimum Acceptable**:
- **Acknowledgment of receipt**: Within 2-3 days of submission
- **Timeline for feedback**: Communicated upfront (e.g., "I'll have comments in 3 weeks")
- **Actual feedback delivery**: 3-4 weeks maximum for chapter-length submissions

**Best Practice**:
> "Ideally, your supervisor will contact you within a few days to let you know they've received your draft and provide a timeline for when feedback will be ready."

**Institutional Requirements**:
- Princeton requires PhD students meet with advisors "in no case less than once per semester"
- **Recommended frequency**: Weekly or bi-weekly meetings during active writing

### Communication Standards

**What Students Need**:
1. **Acknowledgment**: "Got your draft, will review by [date]"
2. **Progress updates**: If timeline changes, communicate proactively
3. **Availability**: Clear office hours or communication windows
4. **Response time expectations**: Agreed-upon timeline (e.g., 48 hours for emails)

### Meeting Frequency Guidelines

**Recommended Schedule**:
- **Early stage (Year 1-2)**: Weekly or every 2 weeks
- **Mid-program**: Every 2-3 weeks
- **Writing stage**: Weekly or bi-weekly check-ins
- **Near completion**: More frequent as needed

**Rationale**:
> "This frequency gives enough time for students to accomplish something but not so much that they waste time or become frustrated going down a bad path."

```
  ‚òê Contact DGS (Director of Graduate Studies)

```

#### 2. **Document Everything**

- **Keep email records** of all submissions
- **Note submission dates** and expected response dates
- **Track meeting attendance** (when scheduled, when actually met)
- **Save all communications** for potential escalation

#### 3. **Set Interim Deadlines**

When submitting work:
> "Hi Professor, I'm submitting Chapter 3 for your review. Based on our previous discussions, would you be able to provide feedback within 3 weeks? I'd like to discuss your comments in our meeting on [date]. Please let me know if this timeline works for you."

**Benefits**:
- Creates collaborative deadline
- Shows professionalism
- Easier to follow up if deadline passes

#### 4. **Follow Up Strategically**

**Timeline**:
- **Day 3-5**: Email to confirm receipt (if no acknowledgment)
- **Week 3**: Polite follow-up about timeline
- **Week 5**: More direct follow-up, copy DGS if appropriate
- **Week 6+**: Escalate to department leadership

**Sample Follow-Up Email**:
```
Subject: Following up: Chapter 3 submitted [Date]

Dear Professor [Name],

I wanted to follow up on Chapter 3, which I submitted on [date]. I know
you're very busy, and I wanted to check if you've had a chance to review
it. I'm eager to incorporate your feedback and move forward with the
next chapter.

Would it be helpful to schedule a brief meeting to discuss your initial
thoughts, even if you haven't had time for detailed comments?

I'm available [times]. Please let me know what works for you.

Best regards,
[Your name]
```

#### 5. **Seek Alternative Feedback Sources**

**While waiting for advisor**, get interim feedback from:

**Free Options**:
- **Committee members**: Ask if they're willing to provide interim feedback
- **University writing center**: Free consultations on structure and clarity
- **Peer writing groups**: Fellow PhD students who understand your struggle
- **Online communities**: PhinisheD, r/GradSchool for advice and support

**Paid Options** (if budget allows):
- **Dissertation coaches**: $40-90/hour for expert guidance
- **Professional reviewers**: GradCoach (~$400-600 per chapter)
- **AI tools**: Thesify, Paperpal ($200-400/year) for instant feedback

**Budget-Friendly Hybrid**:
- Free writing center + AI tool ($20-40/month) = comprehensive feedback while waiting

### Escalation Path

If advisor remains unresponsive:

**Step 1: Direct Conversation**
- Request formal meeting to discuss communication expectations
- Express concern about timeline
- Ask about preferred communication method

**Step 2: Involve Director of Graduate Studies (DGS)**
- Explain situation professionally
- Show documentation of attempts to communicate
- Ask for guidance on next steps

**Step 3: Department Chair**
- If DGS doesn't resolve issue
- Request mediation or advisor change

**Step 4: Graduate School Dean's Office**
- Formal complaint if needed
- Ombudsperson if available
- Document everything

**Step 5: Consider Changing Advisors**
- Last resort but sometimes necessary
- Review department and graduate school guidelines
- Protect yourself legally and academically
